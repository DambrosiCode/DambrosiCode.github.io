Next we have a problem: because the website we scraped this data from took user input, subject names aren't standardized. For instance someone getting their PhD in biology could have said "biology" or "biological science". As humans we know these are the same subject, but to a literal computer these are separate subjects, and since we want to train the computer with supervised learning we should make the groups as homogeneous as possible.

Now we could go through each entry, and give it our own category. But there's over 3,000 rows of data and who has that kind of time? Instead we're going to use Levenshtein distance by way of the fuzzywuzzy package. The Levenshtein distance compares two strings and gives a nice numerical output on "like" they are. For instance "Python" and "Python" have a distance of 0, while "Monty" and "Python" have a distance of 6. You can read more about it here.

The fuzzywuzzy package is based on the the Levenshtein distance, and allows us to get the closest matches of our target string and a list of strings. What we're going to do is collect all of the unique subjects and find their nearest subject based on our chosen parameters. So while "biology", "bio", and "biological sciences" are all objectively different strings, our function will tell the computer that they're close enough to be considered the same, and rename them all to the shortest string, in this case "bio".

            #top 5 matches
        matches = process.extractBests(sort[i], sort, score_cutoff=cutoff, limit = len(sort))
        #index of matches
        indxs = [item[2] for item in matches]
        #match subject and its matched subjects in a dictionary
        sub_dict[subject] = [item[0] for item in matches]
        #remove matches from list
        sort = sort.drop(indxs)
    except:
        pass

Continuing with our preprocessing we're going to do a few more things to clean the data. First we'll eliminate the outliers. Any stipend higher than the 94% and lower than the 80% quantile will be nixed. Next we'll nix the scientific notation so it's readable numbers. And finally we have to do something about the string data.

Because most ML algorithms need numerical data, string inputs won't do. Now we could convert the strings to unique identifiers (IE: "bio"=0, "engineering"=1, "chemistry"=2 etc), but our categories are unique and it doesn't make sense to apply math to them. For instance, if "bio"=0 and "chemistry"=2 half-way between them would be engineering (1). This of course makes no sense, so we have to use one-hot encoding.

The way one-hot works is by creating a matrix of 1s and 0s, and columns of our variables. For example if we had the categories "bio", "chemistry", and "engineering" a one-hot encoded table would look like this

bio chemistry engineering
1 0 0
0 1 0
0 0 1

Where the 1s denote the value existing, and 0s the opposite.

Lastly we'll split the data into training and testing (80/20) sets to prevent over-fitting.

Next up is the actual metrics. Importantly we want to look at R^2 (how close our data is to the slope where 0 unrelated and 1 is perfect relation), Mean Absolute Error (actual error for the overall predictions), and Slope (how perfectly does predicted correlate to actual), and intercept (if the actual stipend is at 0 then what is the predicted). I am also going to graph the Predicted v/ Actual stipends.

A perfect result would have a slope of .5, and an R^2 of 1, which would mean that P perfectly correlates to A. Somewhat unintuitively this is not what we want, as a perfect result would imply overfitting. We want this to be close to zero, but really we're comparing relative to our other models, where the lowest will be the best model for our data.

For the most part this seems to be a decent model. The intercept tells us that the predicted values are a little biased to be higher than the actual, but it's not overly misleading.
